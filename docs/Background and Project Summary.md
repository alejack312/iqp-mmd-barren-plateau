### **Background**

Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) are a broad framework in which a parameterized quantum circuit is optimized to minimize a loss function, typically defined as the expectation value of one or more observables. These methods underpin many proposals in quantum machine learning and quantum optimization. A particularly compelling direction is quantum generative modeling, where the goal is to learn an unknown probability distribution from data and generate new samples from it. The hope is that quantum circuits may offer an advantage in such tasks because certain quantum architectures are believed to be classically hard to sample from.

One important example is the class of Instantaneous Quantum Polynomial-time (IQP) circuits. Complexity-theoretic results show that efficiently sampling from the output distribution of IQP circuits would imply unlikely consequences in computational complexity theory, such as a collapse of the Polynomial Hierarchy, under widely believed assumptions . This suggests that IQP circuits may provide a form of quantum advantage at the level of sampling. Motivated by this, researchers have proposed using IQP circuits as generative models, with the intuition that if quantum sampling is hard to simulate classically, then quantum generative models may represent distributions that are classically difficult to reproduce.

However, a major obstacle in training variational quantum circuits is the barren plateau problem. For many random circuit architectures, the variance of gradients decays exponentially with the number of qubits. In practice, this means that estimating the loss function or its gradients with sufficient precision requires an exponentially large number of measurements (shots), making training infeasible at scale. This challenge has significantly limited the practical scalability of quantum generative models.

A recent development proposes a way around this issue for IQP circuits . The key observation is that while sampling from IQP circuits is believed to be classically hard, certain expectation values required for training—specifically those appearing in Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) loss functions—can be computed efficiently using classical algorithms. This leads to a “train on classical, deploy on quantum” paradigm: parameters are optimized entirely on classical hardware, and only after training are they deployed on a quantum device for sampling. This raises important conceptual questions about how classical training is compatible with quantum sampling hardness and whether the barren plateau phenomenon still arises in this setting.

### **Research Project and Goal**

The goal of this project is to investigate the barren plateau problem in the specific context of IQP circuits used for generative modeling. While the classical-training approach suggests that large-scale optimization may be feasible, it remains unclear whether the underlying loss landscape for IQP-based generative models still exhibits exponential concentration phenomena as the number of qubits increases. In other words, even if gradients can be computed classically, does their variance decay exponentially with system size, or does the special commuting structure of IQP circuits prevent this?

The project aims to analyze and better understand this question, potentially by studying gradient variance scaling, concentration of measure effects, and the structure of the MMD loss landscape for IQP circuits. By clarifying whether barren plateaus persist or are mitigated in this setting, the project seeks to contribute one step toward understanding whether IQP-based quantum generative models can be both trainable at scale and capable of delivering a genuine sampling advantage at inference time.
