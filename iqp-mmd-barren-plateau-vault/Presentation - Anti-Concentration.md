## Slide 1 — What is anti-concentration?

> [!note] One-sentence definition
> A probability distribution over $\{0,1\}^n$ is **anti-concentrated** if a **constant fraction** of the $2^n$ outcomes each carry **at least a constant multiple of the uniform probability** $1/2^n$. In plain words: the mass is spread out, not piled up on a few outcomes.

Three mental reference points (sand in $2^n$ boxes):

| Distribution | Sand shape | Anti-concentrated? |
|---|---|---|
| Uniform | Every box gets $1/2^n$ | Yes — the boundary case |
| Delta | One box gets all of it | No — maximally concentrated |
| Random-$\theta$ IQP | Mass spread over a constant fraction of boxes | Yes (typically) |

Key slogan: **concentrated = "a few outcomes hog all the probability"; anti-concentrated = "many outcomes each have a fair share."**

## Slide 2 — The two definitions (straight from the paper)

Let $p_C$ be the output distribution of a circuit $C$ on $\{0,1\}^n$, and let $x$ be drawn uniformly. The paper states them in two equivalent forms ([arXiv:2512.24801](https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.24801), Section II.B, Eqs. 2–3):

> [!important] Definition A — threshold form
> $$\Pr_{x \sim U}\!\left[p_C(x) \ge \frac{y}{2^n}\right] \ge \beta \qquad \forall\, x;\; y,\beta>0$$
> A constant fraction $\beta$ of bitstrings carry at least $y$ times the uniform probability $1/2^n$.

> [!important] Definition B — second-moment form
> $$2^{2n}\, \mathbb{E}_{x \sim U}\!\left[p_C(x)^2\right] \ge \beta' \qquad \beta' > 1$$
> The scaled second moment exceeds 1 by a constant.

The two are equivalent because the second moment is minimized by uniform, and any "spreadness" mass bound translates between a counting statement (A) and a norm statement (B).

**Physical intuition from the paper:** anti-concentrated means $p_C(x) \in \Theta(2^{-n})$ for typical $x$ — i.e. almost but not exactly uniform. The paradigmatic example is a Porter–Thomas distribution.

## Slide 3 — The function you'd write

> [!tip] Three lines of actual math
> 1. $\hat\beta(\alpha) = 2^{-n}\,\big|\{x : p(x) \ge \alpha \cdot 2^{-n}\}\big|$ — the finite-$n$ count form
> 2. scaled second moment $= 2^n \sum_x p(x)^2$ (identically $2^{2n}\,\mathbb E_x[p^2]$)
> 3. Compare both against thresholds.

```python
def check_anti_concentration(p, alpha=1.0, beta_min=0.25):
    n = int(round(math.log2(len(p))))
    beta_hat = float(np.mean(p >= alpha * 2**-n))            # Def A (finite n)
    scaled_second_moment = (2**n) * float(np.sum(p**2))      # Def B
    return {
        "beta_hat": beta_hat,
        "scaled_second_moment": scaled_second_moment,
        "passes_threshold":     beta_hat >= beta_min,
        "passes_second_moment": scaled_second_moment >= 1.0,
    }
```

The repo's real implementation ([`src/iqp_bp/experiments/run_validation.py:82`](../src/iqp_bp/experiments/run_validation.py)) just adds a grid of alphas and a few convenience scalars:

- `collision_probability` $= \sum_x p(x)^2$ (unscaled second moment)
- `max_probability_scaled` $= 2^n \max_x p(x)$
- `effective_support` $= 1/\sum_x p(x)^2$ (a.k.a. participation ratio)
- `effective_support_ratio` $=$ effective_support $/\, 2^n$

Where $p(x)$ comes from, for an IQP circuit: enumerate $z \in \{0,1\}^n$, build the phase vector $d(z) = e^{-i\sum_j \theta_j (-1)^{z\cdot g_j}}$, apply a Walsh–Hadamard transform, square. That's exactly `IQPModel.probability_vector_exact()` ([`src/iqp_bp/iqp/model.py:108`](../src/iqp_bp/iqp/model.py)).

## Slide 4 — Example run: uniform vs. delta vs. random-$\theta$ IQP

Script: [`scripts/explore_anti_concentration.py`](../scripts/explore_anti_concentration.py) at $n=3$. For the random-$\theta$ case I used the `complete_graph` family on 3 qubits with seed 42.

> [!example] Side-by-side diagnostics ($n=3$, $2^n=8$)
> | Metric | Uniform | Delta | Random-$\theta$ IQP |
> |---|---:|---:|---:|
> | `scaled_second_moment` ($2^n\sum p^2$) | **1.000** | 8.000 | **3.063** |
> | `max_probability_scaled` ($2^n \max p$) | 1.000 | 8.000 | 4.059 |
> | `effective_support` ($1/\sum p^2$) | 8.000 | 1.000 | 2.611 |
> | `beta_hat(0.5)` | 1.000 | 0.125 | 0.500 |
> | `beta_hat(1.0)` | **1.000** | **0.125** | **0.250** |
> | `beta_hat(2.0)` | 0.000 | 0.125 | 0.250 |
> | `passes_primary_threshold` ($\alpha=1,\ \beta_{\min}=0.25$) | True | **False** | True |

**Read those numbers out loud:**

- **Uniform** — boundary case. Scaled second moment is exactly 1; $\hat\beta(1.0) = 1$. Passes both.
- **Delta** — scaled second moment is $2^n$, so it **"passes"** the naive second-moment check, but $\hat\beta(1.0) = 1/2^n$ nails the failure. This is the punchline: **report both diagnostics**, because the second-moment form alone is fooled by spikes.
- **Random-$\theta$ IQP** — roughly a quarter of bitstrings sit above the uniform threshold, effective support is about $2.6$ out of $8$. It's noticeably less flat than uniform, but it's clearly anti-concentrated, matching the "typical IQP is almost uniform" picture in the paper.

Also shipped the full pipeline on a 6-qubit complete-graph IQP (see `results/validation/complete_graph_n6_seed7.json`):

- `scaled_second_moment = 6.24` → strongly above 1, passes.
- `primary_beta_hat = 0.234 < 0.25` → **fails the primary threshold** with $\beta_{\min} = 0.25$. Interpretation: at $n=6$ a single instance is noisy; averaging over random $\theta$ seeds would be the honest thing to do for a real claim.

## Slide 5 — How this ties to IQP sampling hardness

**The quantum advantage chain** (paper §II.B):

1. IQP hardness proofs (Bremner–Jozsa–Shepherd) encode a #P-hard problem into $p_C(x)$.
2. Via Stockmeyer, a classical $\epsilon$-sampler for $p_C$ would give a multiplicative estimate of $p_C(x)$ and collapse the polynomial hierarchy.
3. **That argument uses anti-concentration as a hypothesis**: without it, "multiplicative" and "additive" precision are interchangeable, and additive estimation of $p_C(x)$ to $1/\mathrm{poly}(n)$ precision is classically tractable (the paper cites an efficient classical algorithm for diagonal IQP circuits).

**So for our "train on classical, deploy on quantum" project:**

- If training an IQP circuit preserves anti-concentration → the quantum sampling step is (plausibly) still classically hard, and the deploy step has a point.
- If training concentrates $q_\theta$ into a handful of spikes → a classical machine can just guess the spikes and simulate the sampler. The quantum advantage is gone regardless of how well the circuit learned the data.

**But also the paper's twist** (from the abstract): *models that anticoncentrate are not trainable on average*. That is the tension at the heart of the project — anti-concentration is necessary for hardness but it also implies loss-function concentration. The goal of this presentation is to define and measure the quantity; the goal for the next few weeks is to measure it **on trained IQP checkpoints** and see which side of the tradeoff we land on.

> [!question] Open question for the supervisor
> After training on a concrete target (e.g. product Bernoulli or a small Ising dataset), do the learned IQP distributions still satisfy $2^n \sum p^2 \approx \text{const}$ and $\hat\beta(1.0) \ge 0.25$? That's the exact check the deterministic validation pipeline is now ready to answer.
